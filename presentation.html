How we got agreement

1. Made the disagreement visible

Ran all 4 formulas on real data. Showed the same customer with 4 different values. Nobody could ignore it.

2. Named each method honestly

Revenue CLV, Gross Margin, Net Margin, Predictive — each has a valid use case. No one was "wrong."

3. Picked a canonical default

Gross margin as the exec default: reflects real product economics without over-allocating shared costs. Others are one toggle away.

Quality & trust

Testing approach

Edge thinking, evaluation, and reversal thinking — because the whole point is trusting the numbers.

⚠

Edge thinking

What breaks at the boundaries?

Zero orders — customers with no purchases return CLV=0, not an error
NULL StandardCost — 12 products lack cost data. Flagged, not silently dropped
Single-order lifespan — floors to 1 year. No division by zero in predictive
Negative net CLV — valid! Freight > margin happens. Don't filter it out
SQL injection via query_data() — only SELECT/WITH allowed
☑

Evaluation

Do the numbers make mathematical sense?

Revenue ≥ Gross margin — always true. Cost can't be negative
Gross ≥ Net — freight + tax only subtracts, never adds
Reconcile covers 100% — every customer in revenue appears in reconciliation
What-if monotonic — lower freight always increases net CLV
Predictive bounds — flagged when predictive > 3x historical (noisy extrapolation)
↺

Reversal thinking

If we're wrong, what would prove it?

What if gross > revenue? — Should never happen. If it does, discount calc is wrong
What if all 4 agree? — Suspicious. Likely means freight/tax/cost are all near-zero
What if the "best" customer is negative? — Means our revenue ranking masks a money-loser
What if predictive CLV = 0? — Lifespan flooring failed or orders missing
What if reconciliation shows no drivers? — Threshold tuning needed; add "broadly aligned" fallback
◆

7 automated tests in pytest covering all edge cases above. Every test maps to a real scenario we found in AdventureWorks data — these aren't hypotheticals.
